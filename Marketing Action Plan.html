🔥 Critical Brand ⚠ Conflict Resolved
Audit the brand name — resolve the CALtrack conflict before anything else

One review flagged that "CALtrack" already exists as an established Australian industrial calibration software brand (active since the 1990s). Before spending another dollar on marketing, design, or content, you need to know if this is a real problem.

  • Search IP Australia (ipaustralia.gov.au) for "CALtrack," "Caltrack," and "CalTrack" trademarks in Class 42 (software)
  • Google "CALtrack calibration software Australia" and review what comes up
  • Check if there is an existing domain, LinkedIn page, or active website for any competitor using this name
  • If there is active brand presence in your direct category, take it seriously — the risk is SEO dilution, buyer confusion, and potential legal challenge down the line
  • If the name is genuinely problematic: start a brand name shortlist process (a separate exercise — do not rush this)
Do not launch marketing campaigns, run paid ads, or commission brand assets until this is resolved. Rebranding after market traction is expensive and disruptive.

🔥 Critical Marketing
Write a single, sharp positioning statement — and lock it before touching the website

Everything downstream (headlines, CTAs, LinkedIn posts, sales conversations, demo scripts) flows from this one sentence. Get it wrong and everything else is misaligned. Both reviews agree you don't have one yet.

Use this template to draft it:

"For [specific person] at [specific type of company] who [has this problem], [product name] is the [category] that [delivers this outcome] — unlike [named alternative] which [falls short in this way]."

Recommended draft to refine:

"For instrumentation and maintenance teams at mid-market Australian process plants who are managing calibration in spreadsheets or basic CMMS modules, [Product Name] is the purpose-built calibration intelligence platform that gives them real-time visibility of overdue instruments, drift trends, and full calibration history — without the complexity and cost of enterprise software."

Test it: does it say who it's for? Does it name the alternative? Does it say what's different? If yes to all three, it's done.

Everything in your marketing is downstream of this sentence. Write it on a Post-it and stick it above your monitor.

🔥 Critical Tech/SEO
Fix the technical SEO foundations — your site may currently be invisible to Google

The second review flagged that the site appears JavaScript-heavy and the page content was not meaningfully readable by a browser crawler. This means Google may not be indexing your content properly.

  • Ensure every page has a unique, descriptive <title> tag (not just "Caltrack Pro" on every page)

  • Add basic structured data: SoftwareApplication schema, Organisation schema
If your site content lives inside JavaScript and isn't server-side rendered, Google may be seeing a blank page. Fix this before investing in any SEO content.

🔥 Critical Marketing
Rewrite the homepage using a pain-first structure

Both reviews agree the current homepage leads with the product instead of the buyer's pain. The rewrite should follow this exact structure:

  • Hero: A headline that names the pain, not the product. Subheadline that names the outcome. Two CTAs: "Book a demo" and "See how it works." No feature list here.
  • Pain section: Call out the mess explicitly — overdue instruments in spreadsheets, scattered PDFs, no drift visibility, audit stress, records lost when people leave
  • Outcome section: What life looks like after — not features, but outcomes. "Know your entire calibration status at a glance." "Find any instrument's full history in 10 seconds."
  • Product proof: 3–4 screenshots of real product UI (not mockups). Short captions in outcome language.
  • IXOM case study snippet: One quote, one outcome metric, link to full case study
  • Pricing signal: Even "Starting from $X/month" or "Site-based pricing from $X" — something so buyers don't bounce assuming enterprise cost
  • Final CTA: "Book a 20-minute demo" or "Start a free 30-day pilot"

Use industrial language throughout: "pressure transmitters," "flow meters," "loop calibration," "as-found / as-left" — not generic "equipment" or "assets."

CONSENSUS: Both reviews flagged this independently. The homepage is your 24/7 sales rep. It must earn trust in under 10 seconds.

Marketing Commercial
Write and rehearse the demo script — same structure every time, customised at the edges

A great demo is not an improvised product tour. It is a structured narrative with a beginning (their pain), middle (your solution), and end (next step). Both reviews noted the demo flow needs to be deliberate.

The five-step structure to use:

  • Open with their situation: "How are you currently tracking calibration due dates?" — let them talk for 2 minutes. This tells you what to emphasise.
  • Show the overdue dashboard first: "Right now, this site has 14 overdue instruments and 3 with drift warnings." This is the emotional hook — they've never seen their problem displayed this clearly.
  • Drill into one instrument: Click into a specific instrument. Show the full calibration history, the drift trend graph, the last technician who touched it. Engineers will lean forward here.
  • Show scheduling and alerts: How does the system notify the right person before something goes overdue? Keep this brief.
  • Close on speed of deployment: "We can have your first 100 instruments loaded and your team using this within a week." This is your kill shot vs. every enterprise alternative.

Total demo time: 20–25 minutes. Leave 10 minutes for questions.

Phase 5

Product Gaps — Fix the Critical Missing Features

Ongoing · Product investment priorities to unlock sales
22
🔥 Critical Product
Investigate mobile / field access — this may be your most important product gap

Both reviews flagged this independently. Instrumentation work happens in the field — at the instrument, not at a desk. If technicians can't access or update calibration records from a tablet or phone in the field, you will lose deals to Beamex and others who have this.

  • Talk to 5 instrumentation technicians about their current field workflow — how do they record calibration results in the field today? (Paper, tablet, phone, nothing?)
  • Minimum viable: a mobile-responsive web interface that lets a technician look up an instrument, log a calibration result, and mark a job complete from a phone on-site
  • Full feature: a native or PWA app with offline capability (for sites with poor connectivity) and camera/barcode scanning
  • Do not invest in this before validating with real technicians what they actually need in the field
Every enterprise competitor has mobile/field capability. If you don't, this will surface as an objection in demos. Know your answer before it comes up.
23
Product
Build a spreadsheet import wizard — this is the single biggest adoption lever

Both reviews called this out. If a new customer has to manually re-enter their 200 instruments one by one, they will not complete onboarding. The import experience determines whether pilots succeed.

  • Build a CSV import that accepts a simple format: tag number, description, location, calibration frequency, last calibration date, next due date
  • Provide a downloadable template so customers know exactly what format to use
  • Include validation that catches common errors (blank tag numbers, invalid dates) before import completes
  • Show a preview before committing the import
  • After import: take the customer straight to the overdue dashboard so they immediately see value
A customer who completes import and sees their data in the product within the first session has a 5–10x higher conversion rate than one who doesn't get that far.
24
Product Commercial
Build calibration certificate / report export — non-negotiable for compliance customers

Industrial buyers who care about compliance (which is most of them) need to be able to produce a calibration certificate or report on demand — for auditors, for their clients, for their QA system. If you can't produce a document from the system, you'll lose compliance-conscious buyers.

  • At minimum: a PDF export of a single instrument's calibration history, formatted professionally
  • Better: a configurable calibration certificate template (company name, site, instrument details, as-found/as-left readings, technician name, date)
  • Best: an "audit pack" one-click export — all records for a specified period, site, or instrument group in a single PDF
  • This also handles the "what do I show the auditor?" question that comes up in almost every enterprise sales conversation
25
Product
Add instrument criticality ranking — a high-value differentiator the market doesn't emphasise

The second review specifically highlighted this as a high-value differentiator. Maintenance and reliability engineers think in terms of criticality — a failed level transmitter on a process-critical vessel is a different risk to a temperature indicator on a utility line. Your software should reflect this.

  • Simple version: a criticality field (High / Medium / Low) that can be assigned per instrument, and used to sort the overdue dashboard by risk rather than just date
  • Better: a structured criticality assessment that considers loop function, consequence of failure, and redundancy
  • The headline this unlocks: "Prioritise your overdue instruments by risk — not just by date." No competitor in the mid-market says this clearly.
26
Product
Ensure user roles and permissions are solid before any pilot customer goes live

Industrial sites have hierarchy. A technician should be able to log calibration results. A supervisor should be able to approve and sign off. A read-only viewer (plant manager, auditor) should see everything but change nothing. If permissions aren't in place, you'll face an objection in the first week of every pilot.

  • Minimum roles: Technician (enter calibration results), Supervisor (approve, edit, manage instruments), Viewer (read-only), Admin (full access including users)
  • Each role should have clear access boundaries — no technician should be able to delete instruments or change calibration intervals
  • Test all roles before going live with any pilot customer
27
Product
Roadmap the next-wave features — communicate them clearly to pilot customers

Pilot customers will ask about features you don't have yet. Having a clear, honest roadmap — and sharing it — builds trust and keeps pilots engaged through the conversion decision.

Features both reviews identified as high-value next wave:

  • Barcode / QR code scanning for instrument identification in the field
  • As-found / as-left structured forms for compliant record-keeping
  • CMMS integration (MEX first — that's the platform your origin customer used)
  • Contractor/vendor calibration records (who did it if not internal team)
  • Multi-site hierarchy for customers with more than one plant
  • Electronic signatures (for highly regulated customers — this is a later feature)

Share the roadmap in pilot onboarding. Frame it as: "Here's what's live now. Here's what's coming. Your feedback influences what we prioritise."

Marketing ⚠ Conflict Resolved
Define your ICP exclusions — be clear about who you are NOT selling to right now

The second review suggests pharmaceutical and biotech as a secondary ICP. This is not wrong for the long term — but it's wrong for right now. Here's the clear ruling:

Do NOT pursue these segments in the next 12 months:

  • Pharmaceutical / biotech: Requires 21 CFR Part 11 compliance, electronic signatures, full validation documentation (IQ/OQ/PQ), and a completely different sales cycle involving QA managers and regulatory affairs teams. Your product isn't ready and your sales motion isn't set up for it.
  • Aerospace / defence: AS9100, NADCAP — similar story. Long procurement cycles, strict security requirements.
  • Very large enterprise (1,000+ instruments, multi-country): Your implementation capacity doesn't support this yet without dedicated customer success resources.

Your ICP right now is: Mid-market Australian process plants (chemical, water/wastewater, mining processing, food & beverage, oil & gas) with 50–500 instruments, currently using Excel or weak CMMS calibration modules, where a site manager can approve a $1,000–2,000/month spend without a lengthy procurement process.
